\noindent\textbf{6.} We first prove that $E'$ is closed. Suppose not,
then there exists a limit point $x$ to $E'$ such that $x \notin E'$. For
any neighborhood $N_r(x)$ with radius $r > 0$ there is a $y \in E'$ such
that $y \in N_r(x)$. Since $y \in E'$ we know that $y$ is a limit point to
$E$. Hence we can find a $z \in E$ such that $z \in N_{r-h}(y)$ with $r > h >
0$. We have that
\begin{align*}
    d(z, x) \leq d(z, y) + d(y, x) < r - h + h = r,
\end{align*}
which means that $z \in N_r(x)$. Since $r > 0$ is arbitrary it follows
that $x$ is a limit point to $E$. But this is a contradiction since it
would imply $x \in E'$. Therefore our assumption is wrong and $E'$ must
be closed.

Next we show that $\overline{E}$ and $E$ has the same limit points. If
$x$ is a limit point to $\overline{E}$, then since $\overline{E} = E'
\cup E$ it must be the case that $x$ is either a limit point to $E'$ or
$E$. Assuming $x$ is a limit point to $E$ leaves us with nothing to
prove. So we suppose that $x$ is a limit point to $E'$ alone. We
already have established that $E'$ is closed and therefore $x \in
E'$. This in turn directly implies that $x$ is a limit point to $E$
because the members of $E'$ are all limit points to $E$. Conversely,
if $y$ is a limit point to $E$ then we know that $y \in E'$. Since
$\overline{E} = E' \cup E$ it follows that $y \in \overline{E}$ and is a
limit point to $\overline{E}$.

Lastly we show that $E$ and $E'$ do not always have the same limit
points. Let $E = \{ 1/2, 1/3, \dots \}$ and note $0$ is the only limit
point which means that $E' = \{ 0 \}$. It is evident that $E'$ has no
limit points which shows the result by example.
