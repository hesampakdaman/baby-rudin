\noindent\textbf{7.} a) We first need to show that for any sets $A$
and $B$ it is true that $(A \cup B)' = A' \cup B'$. 

If $x \in (A \cup B)'$, then for every neighborhood $N_r(x)$ with a radius
$r > 0$ there exists at least one point $p \in N_r(x)$ such that $p \in (A
\cup B)$. Thus $p \in A$ or $p \in B$ which implies that $x \in A'$ or $x \in B'$
so that $x \in A' \cup B'$.

Suppose now that $x \in A' \cup B'$. Then for any neighborhood $N_r(x)$ with
a radius $r > 0$ there is a point $p \in N_r(x)$ such that $p \in A$ or $p
\in B$. This means that $p \in A \cup B$ and it follows that $x \in (A \cup B)'$.

We turn now to the question at hand. This will be a proof by
induction. The base case for $n=1$ is clearly true. Assume the
statement holds for $n$, then
\begin{align*}
        \bar{B}_{n+1} 
        =& \overline{\bigcup_{i=1}^{n+1} A_i}
        = \overline{\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} A_i \cup A_{n+1}}
        = \overline{B_n \cup A_{n+1}} \\
        =& B_n \cup A_{n+1} \cup (B_n \cup A_{n+1})' \\
        =& B_n \cup B_n'  \cup A_{n+1} \cup A_{n+1}' \\
        =& \bar{B}_n \cup \bar{A}_{n+1}
        = \bigcup_{i=1}^{n} \bar{A}_i \cup \bar{A}_{n+1}\\
        =& \bigcup_{i=1}^{n+1} \bar{A}_i, 
\end{align*}
where we have used the induction hypothesis in the penultimate
equality. \\



\noindent b) If $x \in \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty \overline{A}_i$ then there is an $n \in
\mathbb{N}$ such that $x \in \overline{A}_n$. We have that
\begin{align*}
        \overline{B} &=
        \overline{\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i} \supset 
        \overline{\bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i} =
        \bigcup_{i=1}^n \overline{A}_i \supset 
        \overline{A}_n,
\end{align*}
where we used the result in a) to get the second equality. This
implies that $x \in \overline{B}$ and since $x$ is arbitrary it follows
that $$\overline{B} \supset \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty \overline{A}_i,$$ as desired.

We shall conclude by showing that this subset can indeed be a
proper. Let $A_i = \{1/2, 1/3, \dots, 1/i\}$ and note that there exists no
$n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $0 \in A_i$. Thus $0 \notin \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty
A_i$. The set of limit points to $A_i$ is the empty set which is why
$\overline{A}_i = A_i$ and therefore $\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty \overline{A}_i =
\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i$. It follows then that $0 \notin \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty
\overline{A_i}$. Now let's consider $\overline{B}$
\begin{align*}
        \overline{B} &=
        \overline{\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i} =
        \overline{ \{ 1/2, 1/3, \dots \} } =
        \{ 0, 1/2, 1/3, \dots \},
\end{align*}
which implies that $0 \in \overline{B}$. Since $0 \notin \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty
\overline{A_i}$ we've shown that the inclusion can be proper.

\hfill$\square$
